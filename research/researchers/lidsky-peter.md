## Research Focus

### Primary Research Areas

#### Pathogen Control Theory of Aging
Prof. Lidsky proposes a paradigm-shifting theory: **aging evolved as an adaptive mechanism to control the spread of pathogens**. This represents a fundamental departure from traditional aging theories that view aging as evolutionary deterioration or byproduct.

**Core Premise**: Aging is not a failure but an evolved protective strategy.

**Testable Predictions**: His team is developing experimental predictions to validate this framework, emphasizing that understanding aging's nature is prerequisite to developing treatments.

**Epistemological Position**: According to his Foresight Institute presentation, this model "should be considered a priority hypothesis" due to its explanatory power and scientific rigor compared to competing theories.

#### Cellular Senescence as Antiviral Defense
His "immune militia" hypothesis conceptualizes cellular senescence not as cellular damage but as an **active antiviral defense mechanism**.

**Connection to CellAge**: This perspective directly relates to our [[../datasets/cellage|CellAge database]] research, where we track 950 genes associated with cellular senescence. Lidsky's framework suggests these genes may be part of evolved pathogen defense systems.

**Relevance to SASP**: The senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) - traditionally viewed as pro-inflammatory and harmful - may actually serve protective immune signaling functions under this model.

#### Virus-Host Interactions (Historical Focus)
Background research in Picornaviruses - a family of RNA viruses including poliovirus, rhinovirus, and hepatitis A. Expertise in how viruses manipulate host cell machinery for replication.

## Connection to Our Research

### Relevance to CellAge Database ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Critical connection**: Lidsky's "immune militia" hypothesis of cellular senescence directly relates to our [[../datasets/cellage|CellAge database]] containing 950 senescence-associated genes.

**Reinterpretation opportunity**: Under Lidsky's framework, genes in CellAge that:
- **Induce senescence** → May be activating antiviral defense
- **Inhibit senescence** → May be suppressing unnecessary immune activation
- **SASP factors** → May be immune signaling molecules, not just inflammatory damage

**Testable hypotheses using CellAge**:
1. Are senescence-inducing genes enriched for antiviral functions?
2. Do senescence-associated genes show evolutionary signatures of pathogen-driven selection?
3. Are there species-specific differences in senescence genes related to pathogen exposure?

### Relevance to GenAge Database ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Our [[../datasets/genage|GenAge database]] (307 human aging genes) could be analyzed through pathogen control lens:
- Are aging-associated genes enriched for immune functions?
