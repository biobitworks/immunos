	<h1>Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS) and Vaccines</h1>	</div>
	<div id="content" class="cdc-page-content cdc-page-offset syndicate container">
		<div class="cdc-dfe-body ">
	<div class="cdc-dfe-body__top ">
		<div class="dfe-section dfe-section--page-summary summary-blurb summary-list" data-section="cdc_vaccine_special_topics_page_summary">
			<div class="dfe-section__header">
		<h2 class="editor-block--title" id="heading-1beinhhaju">Key points</h2>
		</div>
		<div class="dfe-section__content">
		<ul class="nested-list">
<li class="level-1">SIDS is the sudden, unexpected death of a baby younger than 1 year of age that doesn&rsquo;t have a known cause even after a complete investigation.<ul>
</ul>
</li>
<li class="level-1">Multiple research studies and safety reviews have found that vaccines do not cause and are not linked to SIDS.<ul>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
		<div class="dfe-section__nav hide" id="dfe-section__nav">

		<div class="dfe-section" data-section="cdc_vaccine_special_topics_overview">
	<a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="cdc_vaccine_special_topics_overview-overview" name="Overview" title="Overview"><!-- --></a><h2 class="editor-block--title" id="heading-mokcytkgkp">Overview</h2>
<p>Babies receive multiple vaccines when they are between 2 to 4 months old. This age range is also the peak age for <a href="/sids/index.htm">sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)</a>. The timing of the 2 month and 4 month shots and SIDS has led some people to question whether they might be related. However, studies have found that vaccines do not cause and are not linked to SIDS.</p>
<p>Multiple research studies and safety reviews have looked at possible links between vaccines and SIDS. The evidence accumulated over many years do not show any links between childhood immunization and SIDS.</p>

</div>
<div class="dfe-section" data-section="cdc_vaccine_special_topics_recommendations">
	<a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="cdc_vaccine_special_topics_recommendations-steps-you-can-take" name="Steps%20you%20can%20take" title="Steps you can take"><!-- --></a><h2 class="editor-block--title" id="heading-khnichbp_9">Steps you can take</h2>
<div class="dfe-block cdc-callout cdc-callout--message"><div class="cdc-callout__icon" aria-hidden="true" role="img"></div>
<h3 class="cdc-callout__heading">Fact</h3>
<div class="cdc-callout__body"><div class="cdc-callout__content"><p>Placing healthy babies on their backs to sleep reduces the risk of SIDS.</p>
</div>
</div></div>
<p>In 1992, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommended that healthy babies be placed on their backs to sleep. That recommendation, along with the National Institute of Child Health and Human Development's 1994 "Back-to-Sleep" campaign (now known as the <a href="https://safetosleep.nichd.nih.gov/" class="tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="gov">Safe to Sleep&reg; campaign<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external cdc-link-icon" aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a>), encouraged caregivers to place infants on their backs to sleep, and coincided with a dramatic reduction in the SIDS rate in the United States. <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27940804" class="ce-selection tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="gov">See the latest recommendations on a safe infant sleep environment<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external cdc-link-icon" aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a> from the American Academy of Pediatrics.</p>
<p>Also, research has found the rate of SIDS declined dramatically following the 1994 "Back-to-Sleep" campaign, and then stabilized in the 2000s at a time when the number of infant immunizations was increasing. The findings provide strong evidence that immunization is not linked to SIDS. See the published article on SIDS rates.</p>
